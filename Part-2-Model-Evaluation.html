5. MODELING, EVALUATION, AND DEPLOYMENT

5.1 The Goal of our Model

When considering the type of data we have, our business problem, our findings from data exploration, and the nature of our analytical work, our team has decided to go with an a priori machine learning algorithm. What this means is that our model will learn from customers patterns of previously purchased products, and then apply that learned knowledge to any customer profile. We chose this algorithm in order to recommend to the customer the most precise and relevant items that will either keep customer loyalty or add value to their basket/online visit (or both!), which will be based on either their current basket or their historic purchases, if they are a recurring customer.

To perform our analyses and model evaluation, we will use the R package called arules, which is specifically made for apriori learning.

library(arules)

5.2 The Theory behind the Algorithm

This package uses the algorithm called Association Rule Mining to draw insight from a given basket (ie transaction), and developing associations between item sets, essentially creating relationships between frequently bought items. There are generally two steps behind the algorithm:

  1. Frequent Itemset Generation
  2. Rule (Association) Generation

Frequent Itemset Generation essentially creates a candidate list of those items that qualify as frequent enough, by making an itemset lattice. From this lattice there are various sets (ex. {Milk} as one set, or {Milk, Eggs} as another, and {Milk, Bread, Butter} as another) that have relations to one another. The apriori algorithm then helps reduce the number of lattice relations by pruning certain sets and/or supersets that are considered infrequent, thereby creating a candidate list of only relevant item sets. This relevant list will help us generate meaningful rule associations.

For more detailed information on the apriori algorithm please check this documentation: https://dwgeek.com/mining-frequent-itemsets-apriori-algorithm.html/

Before going any further, we must explain some very key concepts in terms of our performance metrics and what we are looking for. There are three (3) key metrics that we need to consider for our data: Support, Confidence, and Lift.

  • Support refers to how many transactions empirically support the given condition.
    • A high support means the condition occurs various times throughout our matrix.
    • A small support means the condition occurs very few times, and thus not a very supported condition.
  • Confidence refers to how certain a condition is. Mathematically speaking, it is the percentage of times that item 1 was bought, given that item 2 was bought.
    • A confidence of 1 means that 100% of customers who bought item 2, also bought item 1.
    • A confidence of 0.5 means that 50% of customers who bought item 1, also bought item 2 (and thus the other 50% of customers who bought item 1, did NOT buy item 2 and bought something else).
  • Lift is how often the condition actually happens compared to the expected chance for that condition to happen. In other words, lift is the ratio of confidence to expected confidence, almost like a correlation. Lift is a good rule of thumb that describes the strength of an association between items, however it is also good to be wary that lift can be inflated due to coincidences, therefore it is always good to look at support and confidence just as much.
    • A lift of 1.0 typically means that the rule/item set is occurring as expected.
    • A lift of 2.0 means that the rule is occuring twice the normal amount that is expected.

It is very interesting to note how vastly different the rules generated are between the two datasets. The default rules produce various associations related to toiletries (paper towel, toilet paper, napkins), bread related purchases, and juices. The new order rules, with a higher minimum support, produce associations such as milk and yogurt, juice items, snacks, salads and dressing. From the quick inspection into the rules, as well as the summary statistics that we have observed, there seems to be varying results based on if we perform our data mining on order transactions or on users transaction. Statistically speaking, the order transactions data seems to have better metrics in terms of lift, and confidence, but this could be due to the fact that order transactions has more random, one-off purchases that occur more than expected, which is a cause of high lift. This can also be explained through the density previously mentioned, where rules based on order has a smaller density. Since user_rules have a higher density, it could provide more meaningful results, especially seeing since our goal is to provide our customers with personalized recommendations to strengthen and maintain their loyalty. Also from our quick inspection, we see that the associations that user_rules created seemed to be more diversified than the order_rules.

As tempting as it may be to continue our analysis using order_rules because it has higher lift and confidence values overall, we must remember to take the lift metric with a grain of salt as the lift metric could perform poorly when there is random noise. As mentioned by this paper, lift has a strong tendency to produce the highest values for rules which just pass the set minimum support threshold (https://michael.hahsler.net/research/hyperConfidence_IDA2007/hyperConfidence.pdf). This is theoretically true for the orders data since there are probably many more one-off purchases that have no connection, whereas the users data will likely have more meaningful item-set connections.

Not to mention, user_rules has many more rules (9319) than order_rules (a mere 70). Therefore, we will continue to refine our model using the transactions based on user filtering.

5.5 Refining Our Model

Now that we have decided to use user_transactions as for our data mining, we will further refine this dataset to users that have more than 5 orders (users that have ordered from us more than 5 times). We choose the number 5, because seeing as once a customers shops at our store more than 5 times, we would naturally consider that as being a loyal customer. We do this in order to get a better idea of our loyal customers shopping habits, since we want to cater our recommendations towards these users.

We will perform the same steps we previously took in order to get the rule associations for our loyal customers.

5.8 Limitations

As with any good model evaluation, we must also discuss its limitations. Though we have already discussed the limitations and drawbacks of our model throughout this report, here we will list some of the more important ones that we must take into consideration. Even though in Section 5.4 we decided to continue with our analysis by applying our algorithm to transactions based on the user_id variable (rather than on the order_id variable), there are still caveats associated with this decision. For one, rules generated based on customer transactions will have a certain bias related to each individual customers shopping habits. Perhaps loyal customers have similar shopping habits in that they might buy a combination of certain items more often than regular customers would, which introduces bias. This bias however, may actually work for us towards achieving our goal and our clients goal, which is to create personalized item recommendations and promote customer loyalty. Another limitation regarding this decision however is that certain extremely loyal customers (perhaps more than 50 orders) could be inflating certain item associations, and thus increasing the lift. An appropriate fix to this could be to choose to filter out these types of customers from our algorithm, or depending on our mandate, create even more personalized recommendations for these extremely loyal customers, as they are sure to be bringing in more revenue to the store than regular customers.

Another limitation that we addressed was the performance metric of lift. As mentioned, sometimes lift may be unreliable or perform poorly given certain transactional dataset conditions - ie. when there is a lot of random noise in the data. However we promptly corrected this limitation by tweaking our model to generate associations for item-sets with a higher minimum support than the default (supp = 0.002). Another way we addressed this was by sorting our rules based on the support and confidence metric as well.

It is safe to say that these findings have reasonably accomplished our initial goal of providing value to our client in terms of ways to increase their revenue and maintaining and strengthening customer loyalty. We have done this by algorithmically deducing major product purchasing patterns for the more loyal customers. We have clearly seen from our association visualizations, that our clients grocery application should immediately notify a customer to buy a tortilla once they buy a combination of brown rice and any common vegetable. Or, cases where the grocery app should tailor recommendations towards the more organic-product-purchasing customers, since we observed that strawberries are an extremely popular product to buy when anything organic-related is bought. Taking it one step further, the item-specific recommendations, where we investigated Milk, are also just as, if not more, useful than the general item-set rules. This is because we could potentially provide our client with all the frequently/highly associated items that are purchased with that one specific item, which is of great value. All things considered, our results have helped our client achieve their goal of increasing revenue by providing insight that can drive decisions regarding specific item sales (if we lower the price of this, but raise the price of this, etc.), the applications page interface and layout (what item should appear next to which), and when to push certain notifications (if high support/confidence/lift, then push this item notification, as an example). The possibilities are endless, and there are certainly more approaches we could implement in our future analyses.
